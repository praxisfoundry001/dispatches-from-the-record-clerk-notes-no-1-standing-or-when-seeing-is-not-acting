Clerk Notes No. 1 — Standing, or: When Seeing Is Not Acting
Hi.
I’m glad you’re here.
This note is about standing — and about something I didn’t understand until I tried to explain it carefully.
What I Thought Standing Was
I used to think standing meant something like this:
* A person has to show they’re the right person to bring a case.
* They need an injury, caused by the defendant, that the court can fix.
That description isn’t wrong. It’s how standing is taught, and how it’s usually summarized. But it always felt thinner than the work standing actually does.
What bothered me was how often courts acknowledge real harm — sometimes in detail — and then dismiss anyway.
Not because the harm didn’t happen. Not because it wasn’t serious. But because standing was missing.
That gap is where I got stuck.
The Question That Wouldn’t Sit Still
If the harm is real, corroborated, and understood — what exactly is the court not allowed to do?
Because standing decisions don’t look like failures of perception. They look like refusals to act.
So the question became structural, not moral:
What kind of limit is standing actually enforcing?
What this note is not
This note does not replace black-letter standing doctrine. It offers a structural reading of what standing is doing once injury, causation, and redressability are already on the table.
Trying the Physiology Lens
Here’s the frame that clarified things in a hurry for me.
For structural purposes, courts function as the civic system’s perceptual and interpretive organ.
They:
* receive complaints and evidence,
* evaluate what happened,
* determine whether a legally cognizable harm exists.
That is perception.
But perception is not command.
Your conscious mind can register hunger, pain, nausea, or anxiety. It can understand what’s happening. It can even know that something should change.
What it cannot do is directly tell your stomach to stop producing acid.
That isn’t neglect.
It isn’t indifference.
It’s a boundary between organs.
Seen this way, standing is not a judgment about whether harm exists.
It’s a rule about whether judicial perception is authorized to trigger institutional action.
What Suddenly Made Sense
Once I saw that, the familiar elements of standing doctrine lined up quickly.
* Injury in fact isn’t about deserving sympathy. It’s about localizing a harm signal the system can recognize as actionable.
* Causation isn’t moral blame. It’s traceability — whether the harm can be routed through the system without distortion.
* Redressability isn’t optimism. It’s competence — whether a judicial decision would actually engage the right institutional mechanism.
And generalized grievances finally clicked:
* A diffuse harm can be perceived.
* It can even be acknowledged as real.
* But without a localized signal, judicial perception has nowhere legitimate to act.
The court isn’t saying “this doesn’t matter.” It’s saying: this is not something this organ can move (when the court functions properly).
What We Get for Free
Once standing is understood this way, several things fall out without extra argument:
* Standing stops looking like gatekeeping and starts looking like boundary enforcement.
* Dismissals for lack of standing can stop reading as evasive and start reading as institutional honesty about limits.
* The discomfort these cases produce makes sense: the system is forced to recognize harm while refusing permission to act on it.
Nothing new was added.
No values were imported.
The doctrine didn’t change.
The explanation just got cheaper.
Where the Boundary Is — and How Duality Expresses
Standing enforces a clean boundary:
 Judicial perception may acknowledge and interpret harm without being authorized to command a remedy.
On one side:
* recognition,
* seriousness,
* explanation,
* legal understanding.
On the other:
* action,
* enforcement,
* institutional command.
Both are preserved. Neither collapses into the other.
Standing is the doctrine that keeps that duality intact.
What Consciousness Can Still Do
One more thing fell into place once I sat with that boundary.
While consciousness can’t directly command an organ — it can seek remedies that are structurally available to it.
You can’t tell your stomach to stop producing acid. But you can take an antacid, adjust your posture, change what you eat, or decide to see a doctor.
Those actions don’t violate the boundary. They respect it.
The same is true for courts. When standing is absent, judicial perception doesn’t become inert. It just changes form.
Courts can still:
* describe the harm clearly,
* explain why a particular remedy is unavailable,
* preserve the record,
* signal where authority does lie,
* and leave paths open for action by other institutions.
Seen this way, a standing dismissal isn’t a refusal to care. It’s an acknowledgment that care has to move through a different channel.
What This Explains — and What It Doesn’t Yet
This framing explains why standing opinions can feel careful, even humane, while still saying no. It explains why courts can describe harm clearly and still decline to act. It explains why standing protects legitimacy without pretending injury isn’t real.
What it doesn’t yet explain — and what I haven’t worked through — is how intent fits into this structure.
That’s next.
For now, I’m left with a clearer picture of what standing is doing:
* Not denying harm.
* Not judging worth.
* But marking the line between seeing and acting.
Once I saw that, I couldn’t quite unsee it. What about you?
