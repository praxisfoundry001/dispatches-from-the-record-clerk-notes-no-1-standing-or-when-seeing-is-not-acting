# Dispatches from the Record: Clerk Notes No. 1 — Standing, or: When Seeing Is Not Acting

## What is this?
A bridge artifact in the civics / public-law lane of the Structural Flow library.

This note is about standing. It offers a structural way to understand why courts can perceive real harm, describe it clearly, and still refuse to act.

## Why should I care?
Standing often feels confusing because courts may acknowledge serious harm and then dismiss anyway.

This note gives a cleaner explanation: standing is not mainly about whether harm exists. It is about whether judicial perception is authorized to trigger institutional action.

## What is the claim?
The claim is narrow and practical:

standing marks a boundary between seeing and acting.

Courts can perceive, interpret, and explain harm without always being authorized to command a remedy. Once that boundary is clear, injury, causation, redressability, and generalized grievance start to line up more cleanly.

## What is this not?
This artifact is not:
- a replacement for black-letter standing doctrine
- a flagship Constitutional Physiology artifact
- a policy proposal
- a complete theory of judicial restraint

It is a readable structural note designed to help cold readers see what standing is doing.

## How was this done?
The note applies a Constitutional Physiology reading frame to standing:
- courts as perceptual and interpretive organs
- standing as a boundary rule
- injury as localized signal
- causation as traceability
- redressability as institutional competence
